The Morality Hidden Inside the Metric

Hanshu Yu

(Jun. 20th, 2026)

Every method has assumptions it states and assumptions it lives by. The stated ones go in the methods section, where reviewers can see them. The lived ones go unspoken, because to write them down would be to expose them to the question they cannot survive: why should we believe that? Algorithmic fairness, as it is practised, runs on two such assumptions. The first is technical, and people will concede it the moment you press. The second is moral, and it tends to be disowned the moment it is named — which is exactly how you know it is the one doing the work.

Take a concrete case to fix ideas. A hiring algorithm recommends men more often than women. An audit slices the data by sex, reports a disparity, and the disparity is real. That number may well be useful. But before anyone calls it “bias”, two questions have already been smuggled past us without being asked aloud: what, exactly, has been measured — and why is this particular difference a wrong? Neither is a question a statistic can answer, and yet the word “bias” answers both at once, quietly, on our behalf.

I should state at the outset what this essay is not, because the argument invites a lazy misreading. It is not a case against caring how goods are distributed, and it is not the claim that disparities do not matter. Nor is it an indictment of everyone who works on fairness — many of the most thoughtful people in the field are uneasy about exactly the move I am about to describe, and have said so. The target is a style of reasoning, not a profession. What the essay asks for is the opposite of indifference: that the moral argument be made in the open, rather than smuggled in under the cover of a measurement. What I want is not less concern with justice but less disguise.

The first assumption: that the number is a fact about people

The first assumption is the easier one to grant, so begin there. The fairness audit dresses in the clothes of statistical measurement. It speaks of rates, of disparities, of gaps between groups; it produces a number with the look and authority of a finding. And here a careful reader will object, rightly, that many fairness metrics are not statistical inferences at all — demographic parity is a deterministic ratio, a simple comparison of two proportions, and no one needs an assumption of independent draws to compute it. Quite so. So let me be clear about what I am and am not claiming. I am not saying the number is mathematically illegitimate. If the dataset is the population you care about, the ratio is exactly what it says it is, and dependence between people does nothing to change the arithmetic. The objection is not to the computation. It is to the interpretation — to the individualistic story we are tempted to read off the number, in which a rate across a group behaves like a fact about its members.

For that story, independence is the convenient fiction. People are not separate draws from an urn. They are embedded in one another: they share neighbourhoods, schools, workplaces, lineages, exposures. The outcome we observe for one is conditioned on the outcomes of those around them. The friend recommends the job; the cousin co-signs the loan; the school cohort moves together into the same handful of futures. The rate may be perfectly correct. What misleads is the individualistic picture we attach to it — the sense that it summarises a property distributed across separate people, rather than a photograph of a structure that binds them. The disparity is real; what it means is exactly what the tidy comparison leaves out.

There is a second independence smuggled in alongside the first, and it is worse, because no amount of statistical sophistication repairs it. The audit treats the label — Black, white, Asian, man, woman — as a clean external axis along which to slice the data, a fixed coordinate that exists prior to the outcome and independently of it. Let me be exact about the objection, because it is easy to misstate. I am not saying one may not condition on a label, nor that race and outcome must be assumed causally independent; of course one can measure a conditional difference, and the more careful corners of the field now map these dependencies explicitly with causal graphs and counterfactuals. The objection is to the illusion underneath all of it: that the label is an external given rather than itself a historical product. Many such categories were built around — or given their present social meaning through — the very distributions we now measure them against; the meaning was shaped by the same processes that shaped who gets the job, the loan, the diagnosis. And no causal diagram repairs that, because the entanglement is not a missing arrow between variables — it is in the variable itself. To hold the label fixed and ask whether the outcome differs across it is to treat as an independent axis something that is entangled, at the root, with the thing being measured. The audit imagines it is measuring whether a process treats pre-existing groups differently. Often it is measuring the shadow of the process that made the groups in the first place.

The clearest case on record is a healthcare triage algorithm studied in 2019, used across the American hospital system to flag patients for extra care. It relied on a proxy that looked not merely reasonable but rigorous: predicted future healthcare costs as a stand-in for health need. The trouble is that cost is not need. Because of long histories of unequal access, a Black patient and a white patient at the same level of illness do not generate the same spending — the Black patient, historically, costs the system less. So the algorithm, faithfully predicting cost, set a higher bar of sickness for Black patients before it would flag them. When the audit arrived, sliced by race, it found exactly this and pronounced the tool “biased”. But the word does less than it seems to. The algorithm had not malfunctioned in the narrow engineering sense, and it had not explicitly used race; it never saw race at all. It did something more revealing: it faithfully learned a proxy already shaped by unequal access, and reproduced the shape with perfect fidelity. The audit believed it had found a defect in a machine. What it had found was the shadow of the process that made the groups — and calling that shadow “bias” let everyone reach for a technical reweighting in place of the far more uncomfortable argument about access and resources that the finding actually implied.

To be precise about the limits of all this: disparities are not illusions, measurement is not impossible, and entanglement does not condemn us to silence. The claim is narrower and harder to dodge. The number that comes out the far end of a fairness audit is doing much less than its grammar implies. It looks like a finding — it has the syntax of one, a magnitude and a comparison and a threshold — but a finding earns its authority from the conditions under which it was produced, and here those conditions do not hold. The number is conditional all the way down, and the conditions are buried.

The second assumption: that a difference is a wrong

So much for the assumption people will grant you. Here is the one they will not.

Suppose, for the sake of argument, that the measurement problem were solved — that we had a number we could trust, a disparity that survived every correction, a gap that was unambiguously real. We are now in possession of a fact: this group receives this outcome at this rate, and that group at that rate, and the rates differ. What follows? On its own: nothing. A difference is a difference. To get from the rates differ to something is wrong requires a further premise, and that premise is not statistical. It is moral. It says: this particular difference, among the infinite differences the world contains, is one that ought not to exist.

The practitioners know this. They are too sophisticated not to. And so a certain technocratic style performs a careful manoeuvre. It does not argue for the moral premise — to argue for it would be to expose it to disagreement, to the awkward observation that not every disparity is an injustice, that some differences are benign and some are even desirable, that the burden of showing a particular gap is wrongful is a real burden that someone ought to discharge. Instead, the moral premise is built into the vocabulary and then disclaimed. The disparity is named “bias”. The gap is named “unfairness”. A toolkit ships a setting that is “bias-aware”, an algorithm that is “fairness-enhancing”. Every one of these words is a moral judgment wearing the coat of a technical term. To call a difference “bias” is already to have decided it is a defect; the word does the condemning so the author does not have to.

And when you press — when you ask, plainly, are you claiming this disparity is unjust? — the manoeuvre completes itself. The author retreats into the methods section and produces the alibi:

The alibi

“We do not claim that any particular disparity is normatively undesirable. We merely provide a measure of bias and a method for its mitigation, should the practitioner deem mitigation appropriate. The normative question is outside the scope of the present work.”

Read it twice. It is a careful piece of having it both ways. The word “bias” did the moral work up front, where the reader is persuaded and the claim travels. But the moment the moral claim is challenged, it is disowned: we only measured, we never judged. I do not doubt that the disclaimer is usually sincere; that is rather the point. The judgment was laundered through the vocabulary, and the launderer need not even know it has happened. You are left arguing with someone who hands you a loaded word and, when it goes off, insists it was only a weather report — and who may well believe it.

This is not rigour. It is the costume of rigour. Genuine rigour would say: here is the disparity; here is my argument that this disparity is wrongful, resting on these premises about desert, or need, or the history that produced it; and here is what I therefore propose. That argument can be examined. It can be refused. It exposes its author to the possibility of being wrong about the thing that actually matters. A good deal of the literature prefers the other path: the disparity arrives pre-condemned, and the condemnation is deniable.

The tell is the asymmetry. The word “bias” is asserted loudly, in the place where it persuades — the title, the abstract, the press release, the policy brief downstream. The disclaimer is whispered, in the place where it protects — the limitations paragraph no policymaker reads. A claim made loudly and retracted quietly is not a claim withdrawn. It is a claim made under cover.

The deepest assumption: that something must be wrong

Go down one more level — and notice that the ground shifts here from philosophy to sociology. The first two assumptions were about what a number can mean; this last one is about the institution that produces it, its grants and publications and prestige. Beneath the unargued premise that this difference is a wrong lies a structural premise, visible not in any argument but in the shape of the enterprise: the assumption that there must be a wrong — that the analyst's task is to find the injustice latent in the data and the method's task is to correct it. The possibility that an allocation might, on examination, be acceptable — that the honest verdict might be no intervention is warranted here — is not one the apparatus is built to return. A bias detector detects. And the incentives tilt the same way the instrument does: a tool whose characteristic output was “no bias requiring correction” would be harder to publish, harder to fund, and harder to build a career upon than one that reliably finds something to fix.

So the enterprise tilts, by construction, toward the discovery of wrongs and the prescription of corrections. And this tilt has a direction that is worth naming without euphemism. Every correction is a redistribution: it moves some good — a rank, a loan, an admission, a flag — from where the process put it to where the analyst thinks it ought to go. That is a consequential act. It has winners and losers. It is exactly the kind of act that, performed by a government, we would expect to be justified, debated, and owned. Performed by a method, under the banner of “fairness”, it arrives pre-justified by the very word that describes it, and its author has already filed the disclaimer that says he never made a normative claim at all.

I find I object less to the redistributions than to the manner of their arrival. When two interests genuinely conflict — when satisfying this group’s claim means denying that group’s — that is a hard problem, an old problem, the problem political philosophy has worked on for two and a half thousand years without solving. It deserves to be met as what it is: a conflict, to be adjudicated in the open, with the costs named and the reasoning exposed. What I cannot abide is the conflict being dressed as a measurement. The morality slogan deployed as though it ended the argument rather than started it. The high ground seized by whoever gets to the word “fairness” first, so that to question the correction is made to look like a defence of injustice, when it may be nothing more than a request that the moral claim be argued rather than assumed.

Notice, too, who is making these decisions. Often the analyst who decides which disparity is a wrong, which group is owed a correction, which redistribution is righteous, is not among those whose lives the decision rearranges. The good being redistributed is rarely their good. The cost of being wrong is rarely their cost. They decide who gets what, under a principle that keeps them fed, in a transaction from which they are themselves exempt. I do not say this to impugn anyone’s sincerity. I say it because a structure in which the deciders are insulated from the consequences of their decisions is a structure we are right to distrust, whatever the decency of the individuals inside it. We have a long history of moral apparatus that served, first of all, the people who operated it.

I said at the start that this was not an argument against caring how goods are distributed, and here is where that promise comes due. The lazy version of my complaint — drop the morality, just optimise — is self-refuting, and I will not make it. There is no value-free place to stand. To say “let us simply make people’s lives better” is already to have chosen a morality; it merely happens to be one I find more honest than most, because it declares itself. The objection is not to moral commitment in fairness work. It is to laundered moral commitment: the verdict that travels under the passport of a measurement, the redistribution that arrives pre-absolved by its own name, the analyst who claims the authority of a scientist and the immunity of one who “never made a normative claim”.

What I am asking for is not less morality but less disguise. Say what you think is wrong, and why. Put the premise on the page, where it can be argued with — that this gap, unlike that one, is a wrong, and here is the account of desert or need or history that makes it so. Own the redistribution as a redistribution, with its winners and its losers and its costs, rather than smuggling it in as the neutral output of a neutral tool. And when the people who will bear the consequences are not in the room, notice their absence, and treat it as a reason for humility rather than a licence for confidence.

Do that, and we can disagree honestly — which is the most a contested question allows. Decline to do it, and the word “fairness” goes on functioning as it functions now: not as the name of a value we are arguing toward, but as the seal on a verdict we were never permitted to question. I would rather argue. The argument is the respect.

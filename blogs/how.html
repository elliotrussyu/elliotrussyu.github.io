When I Get to Know Feminism…

(Apr. 1st 2025)

Before the main text

I often find artists, writers, movie makers and even philosophers are quite useless in an instantaneous manner, especially in today's world where there is less freedom and less ability to think actually. Such people point at problems, record the emotions and sparks in their brains, display the truth, re-make or distort the truth. However, there is no instantaneous influence, because their work must not go beyond some "red-line" settled by the human beings in possession of power. Through the observation from my naive open eyes, even short-term influence is very rare. And they almost never provide a solution.

I do not like it.

But I do think these people and their works are necessary. I like to solve scientific problems, but this short, naive piece of text is merely acting as a recording tape of my brain. I am only an unborn infant in the fields of mathematics, social science and philosophy. While I might be attacking and contradicting myself in the future, this text at least genuinely records the thought process.

Main

What has always confused me about feminism is its lack of a standard definition of common recognition. It is full of terms and ad-hoc facts but missing the links in the middle. It is more like this: in mathematics, you have a theorem about a property of an infinite set, and by running simulations for a (perhaps very large) finite number of cycles, the observed results match the property. But when I seek hard enough for a proof, I cannot really find it apart from the simulations.

For those who know nothing about mathematics, an infinite set is a collection of an infinite number of elements — like counting all the numbers, which you can never finish even if you start from zero and ignore the negatives and the decimals.

But for those who know a bit more about mathematics, we know that our math has been built from axioms — to be precise, the Peano axioms in number theory, and Zermelo–Fraenkel with the Axiom of Choice (ZFC) or von Neumann–Bernays–Gödel (NBG) in set theory.

Then I read about the defining of feminism, and the more definitions I collected the more certain I became that the field was missing its axioms. So I went back to check what mathematics' axioms actually are — not to use them, just to hold them up next to feminism and let the contrast embarrass it.

The contrast embarrassed me instead.

I had been carrying a picture in which ZFC sits underneath arithmetic the way a foundation sits under a house: poured first, with everything built on top of it afterward. This is not what happened. People did mathematics for centuries — real mathematics, proofs that still hold — without anything like ZFC. The axioms came late. And they came not from someone reading off the bedrock but from someone patching a hole: naive set theory lets you build the set of all sets that do not contain themselves, and that set both contains and does not contain itself, and the whole edifice cracks. Russell found this. ZFC was, in large part, the wall built around that crack. The foundation was poured after the house, to stop the house from collapsing into a paradox it had wandered into on its own.

So the foundation was not found. It was built, and built reactively. And — this is the part my own example had already been whispering to me without my listening — it was chosen. I had written ZFC or NBG as if naming two notations for one thing. They are not. They are different commitments, and a mathematician picks. There are others: you can take the Axiom of Choice or refuse it, and refusing it is not a mistake, it is a different mathematics. The foundation is plural, and the selection among the options is made on grounds that are not themselves theorems. Nobody proves that you should adopt choice. You adopt it, or you don't, for reasons that live outside the system you are adopting it into.

And then there is the fact that broke the demand entirely.

There is a sentence — the Continuum Hypothesis, about whether any infinite set sits in size between the counting numbers and the points on a line — that is perfectly meaningful, perfectly mathematical, and that the axioms neither prove nor refute. Not "have not yet proven." Cannot. Gödel and Cohen showed the sentence is independent: you may add it to ZFC, or add its negation to ZFC, and both resulting systems are equally consistent. The axioms shrug. And mathematicians disagree about which way to settle it — they argue for new axioms that would decide it, and the arguments they give are about fruitfulness, naturalness, intuition, the shape of the mathematical world they want to live in. Extra-mathematical grounds, in other words. The exact kind of grounds I had been treating as a symptom of feminism's softness.

I should have seen it coming even in the clean case. I had held up number theory as the place where everything follows from Peano's axioms, and Gödel had already been standing there the whole time to tell me it doesn't: there are truths about the ordinary counting numbers that Peano arithmetic cannot prove. The tidy example was never tidy. I had simply not looked hard enough to notice that my model of rigor was a model of something that does not exist.

So here is what I actually found, and it is not what I went looking for. I went looking for feminism's axioms and discovered that mathematics does not have axioms in the sense I had imagined either. It has a practice that ran for centuries before anyone wrote a foundation down; a foundation that was retrofitted, reactive, and chosen rather than found; rival foundations that disagree; and meaningful questions the foundation cannot settle, settled — when they are settled at all — by argument from outside.

Which means the thing I was demanding of feminism was not "be as rigorous as mathematics." It was "be more rigorous than mathematics." I wanted a definition that was unique, that was discovered rather than decided, and that closed every question in advance. Number theory cannot give me that. I had taken my own misunderstanding of the most rigorous thing I knew and used it as a ruler, and of course every humane body of thought came up short against a ruler with no real markings on it.

The fault was never in the thing I was measuring. It was in the measurement.
